Circulatory System – Overview

Double Circulation

The human circulatory system features a double pump (the heart) and two distinct circuits. Blood returns to the heart between each circuit.

  • Systemic Circuit: Oxygenated blood leaves the left ventricle via the aorta → distributed to body tissues → deoxygenated blood returns to right atrium via venae cavae.
  • Pulmonary Circuit: Deoxygenated blood leaves the right ventricle → lungs (gas exchange) → oxygenated blood returns to left atrium via pulmonary veins.

Key Vessels

  • Pulmonary arteries – carry deoxygenated blood from right ventricle to lungs
  • Pulmonary veins – carry oxygenated blood from lungs to left atrium
  • Aorta and branches – distribute oxygenated blood to body
  • Venae cavae – return deoxygenated blood from body to right atrium
Key rule: Arteries carry blood AWAY from the heart; veins carry blood TOWARD the heart. The pulmonary artery is the exception to the "arteries carry oxygenated blood" rule.

Blood Vessels – Types & Construction

Three Main Types

  • Arteries: Thick, strong, elastic walls. Carry oxygenated blood (except pulmonary artery). Smaller arteries = arterioles.
  • Veins: Thinner walls, contain valves to ensure correct blood direction under low pressure. Carry deoxygenated blood (except pulmonary vein). Smaller veins = venules.
  • Capillaries: Walls are only ONE cell thick (endothelium only) – allows gas and nutrient exchange. Connect arterioles and venules.

Vessel Wall Layers (Arteries & Veins)

  • External elastic membrane
  • Smooth muscle
  • Internal elastic membrane
  • Endothelium (lumen-facing layer)

Precapillary Sphincter

A band of smooth muscle at the entrance to capillaries that regulates blood flow into the capillary bed.

Arteriovenous Shunt (Metarteriole)

A direct connection between arterioles and venules that bypasses capillaries, allowing blood to be rerouted.

Heart

Structure

4 chambers: 2 atria (upper) and 2 ventricles (lower). The left and right sides are separated by the septum.

Primary Function

Blood pumping through rhythmic contractions of atria and ventricles.

Mechanism of Contraction

  • Caused by waves of depolarization and repolarization passing through cardiac muscle cell membranes
  • During depolarization: Na⁺ flows INTO the cell, K⁺ flows OUT
  • Resting membrane potential maintained by the Na-K pump

Heart Rate

  • Normal resting adult: 60–80 beats/min
  • Higher in children, higher in women than men
  • Increases with emotion, elevated ambient temperature, and physical effort

Ejection Fraction (Systolic Volume)

  • Volume of blood pumped per contraction into the systemic circuit
  • Healthy adults at rest: 50–70 mL

Cardiac Output (Minute Output)

  • Total blood ejected per minute by the left ventricle
  • Formula: Cardiac Output = Heart Rate × Stroke Volume
  • Healthy adults at rest: 4–5 L/min

Energetics of Heart Action

  • Volumetric (External) Work: Performed against pressure in aorta/pulmonary artery (P × ΔV)
  • Kinetic (Internal) Work: Performed to introduce kinetic energy to blood (½ρv²)
W = P × ΔV + ½ρv² × ΔV At rest: Volumetric work ≈ 1.1 J/contraction Kinetic work ≈ 0.01 J/contraction Left ventricle: Volumetric = 0.91 J | Kinetic = 0.006 J Right ventricle: Volumetric = 0.15 J | Kinetic = 0.006 J

Heart as a Pump

  • Energy is used for emptying (not filling) chambers
  • NOT a lift-and-force pump — no vacuum created during filling
  • Pressure in chambers is always positive
  • Filling is passive
  • Efficiency: 15% at rest, 40% under stress
  • Remaining energy released as heat (contributes to body temperature maintenance)

Blood Functions

  • Transport: O₂ from lungs to tissues; CO₂ from tissues to lungs; nutrients from digestive tract; metabolic waste removal; hormones, enzymes, antibodies
  • Water balance: Maintains relatively constant water content in tissues via exchange with lymph or urine
  • Temperature regulation: Blood moves towards skin to dissipate excess heat (cooling)
  • Protection (Immune):
    • Leukocytes (white blood cells) fight infection and disease
    • Platelets form clots to stop bleeding

Blood Composition

Plasma (55% of total blood volume)

  • 91% Water
  • 7% Blood proteins: fibrinogen, albumin, globulin
  • 2% Nutrients: amino acids, sugars, lipids
  • Hormones (erythropoietin, insulin, etc.)
  • Electrolytes (sodium, potassium, calcium, etc.)

Cellular Components (45% of total blood volume) – Buffy Coat

  • White Blood Cells (Leukocytes): 7,000–9,000 per mm³ of blood
  • Platelets: 250,000 per mm³ of blood
  • Red Blood Cells (Erythrocytes): ~5,000,000 per mm³ of blood
Hematocrit (HCT/Ht): Percentage of erythrocytes in total blood volume. Measured in Lab #53.

Hemoglobin

Basic Properties

  • Oxygen-binding protein of red blood cells
  • Molecular weight: 64.5 kDa
  • Globular protein with quaternary structure
  • Consists of 4 polypeptide subunits: 2 α-globin + 2 β-globin (α₂β₂)
  • Each subunit MW ≈ 16 kDa
  • Both α and β subunits have α-helical secondary structure

Heme Group

  • Each subunit has a prosthetic group called heme
  • Heme contains a divalent iron ion Fe²⁺ (ferrous)
  • Iron held in a porphyrin ring
  • Porphyrin = 4 pyrrole molecules cyclically linked by methine bridges with Fe²⁺ in the center
  • One Hb molecule can bind 1–4 O₂ molecules

Oxygenation

  • Process = oxygenation (NOT oxidation) — iron oxidation level does NOT change (stays Fe²⁺)
  • Accompanied by conformational changes in Hb structure (subunit movement)
  • Oxyhemoglobin (HbO₂) = Hb bound with O₂
  • Deoxyhemoglobin = Hb not bound with O₂

Blood Oxygen Capacity

  • 100 mL of blood contains 15 g of hemoglobin
  • 1 g of hemoglobin binds approximately 1.34 mL of O₂
  • Maximum oxygen capacity = 20 mL O₂ per 100 mL blood (ratio 1:5)
Max O₂ capacity = 15 g/100mL × 1.34 mL/g ≈ 20 mL O₂/100 mL blood

Pressure Distribution Along Vessels

Pressure is highest in the ventricle and progressively decreases: Ventricle → Aorta → Arteries → Arterioles → Capillaries → Veins → Atrium.

Blood movement is caused by the pressure difference between arterial and venous systems, maintained by the work of the heart.

Effect of Gravity Field

Hydrostatic pressure: P = ρ × g × h ρ ≈ 10³ kg/m³, g ≈ 10 m/s² → P = (100 × h) hPa or P = (75 × h) mmHg Head artery (h = 0.5 m above heart): P = 130 − 50 = 80 hPa Foot artery (h = 1 m below heart): P = 130 + 100 = 230 hPa

This is why a standing person who remains still for too long risks fainting — blood pools in lower extremities.

Laws Describing Blood Flow

1. Laminar Blood Flow (Normal Condition)

  • Concentric parallel layers of blood move along a vessel
  • Vmax at the vessel center; V=0 at vessel wall (friction)
  • Parabolic flow profile when fully developed
  • Characteristic of long, straight blood vessels under steady flow

2. Turbulent Flow (Pathological / Special Conditions)

  • Chaotic, disordered flow with eddies (vortexes)
  • Energy is wasted during vortex flow
  • In physiological conditions: only immediately after semilunar valve closure (→ heart sounds)
  • Also occurs in atherosclerosis (narrowed vessels)
  • Responsible for heart sounds heard via stethoscope
  • Reynolds number (Re) ≈ 2000–4000 for critical velocity
Critical velocity: v_k = (Re × η) / (r × d) Re = Reynolds' number (~2000–4000) η = viscosity coefficient [Ns/m²] r = vessel radius [m] d = blood density [kg/m³] Re < 2000 → Laminar flow Re > 4000 → Turbulent flow

3. Atherosclerosis & Vessel Lumen Changes

Atherosclerotic plaques (calcium, cholesterol, triglycerides) narrow the vessel lumen. Blood speeds up through the narrowing and can detach plaques → risk of stroke or heart attack.

4. Flow Continuity Principle

  • At any cross-section, the same volume of fluid passes per unit time
  • Valid for incompressible, inviscid fluids with no ramifications
  • Smaller cross-section → higher velocity
Flow rate (flux): J = ΔV/Δt [m³/s] A₁ × V₁ = A₂ × V₂ (continuity equation) With branching: J = ΣJₙ and A = ΣAₙ Key ratio: A_capillary / A_aorta = 750 (Total capillary cross-section is 750× aorta area)

5. Bernoulli's Principle

p + hρg + ½ρv² = constant p = static pressure [Pa] h = height above reference [m] ρ = density [kg/m³] v = velocity [m/s] g = gravitational acceleration [m/s²] For horizontal vessel: p + ½ρv² = const → Where velocity is HIGH, pressure is LOW (and vice versa)

Note: Hydrodynamic pressure (½ρv²) is very low vs. static pressure (e.g., aorta: static = 130 hPa, hydrodynamic = 0.8 hPa).

6. Poiseuille's Law

  • Applies to non-turbulent, non-pulsatile flow through uniform straight pipes
  • Flow rate is directly proportional to pressure difference (ΔP)
  • Inversely proportional to tube length (l) and fluid viscosity (η)
  • Directly proportional to the 4th power of radius (r⁴)
J = (π × r⁴) / (8 × η × l) × ΔP Resistance: R = (8/π) × η × (l/r⁴) J = (1/R) × ΔP ⚠️ 2-fold increase in radius → 16-fold DECREASE in resistance (R is very sensitive to vessel radius!)

Vascular Wall Resilience & Resistance

Arterial Walls

  • Large resilience (elasticity) module
  • Always outstretched (under tension)
  • Function as potential energy containers / air chambers
  • Amortize pressure fluctuations from heart action

Venous Walls

  • Easily change volume
  • Act as voluminal containers
  • Venous compartment contains ~70% of total blood volume

Geometric Factor & Law of Laplace

Geometric factor = L/r⁴ Tensional stress = F/L Law of Laplace: P = T/r P = wall surface pressure on blood [Pa] T = tensional stress in wall [N/m] r = vessel radius [m] Small deformations → elastin fibers (elastic) Large deformations → collagen fibers (stiffer)

Pulse

  • Ejection volume at rest: 70 cm³ every 0.8 s
  • Loop resistance causes the aorta to bulge: kinetic energy of blood → potential energy of aortic wall elasticity
  • A pulse wave = wave of elastic deformations in artery walls
Pulse wave velocity: C = F × √(Ed / 2ρr) C = pulse wave velocity [m/s] F = empirical coefficient (0.6–0.7 for human aorta at 95 hPa) E = Young's modulus [Pa = N/m²] d = artery wall thickness [m] ρ = density [kg/m³] r = vessel radius [m]

Systole vs Diastole in Arteries

  • Systole: Heart contracts → blood ejected → artery wall stretches (systolic/pulse pressure wave)
  • Diastole: Heart relaxes → elastic artery wall recoils → maintains diastolic flow

Blood Viscosity

Viscosity force: F = η × A × (Δv/ΔH) [N] η = shear stress / shear rate η = (F/A) / (v/H) [Ns/m²] A = area [m²], H = height [m], v = velocity [m/s]

Factors Affecting Blood Viscosity

  • Vessel diameter: Below 3 μm — viscosity is LOWER (Magnus Effect)
  • Temperature: At 0°C, viscosity is 2.5× higher than at 37°C
  • Plasma protein concentration: Higher proteins → higher viscosity
  • Flow rate: In 3–5 μm vessels: increased velocity → decreased viscosity
  • Hematocrit (HCT): Higher % of erythrocytes → higher viscosity
  • Erythrocyte properties: Size (7 μm) and deformability affect viscosity

Magnus Effect – Axial Accumulation

  • Erythrocytes preferentially migrate to the center of the vessel
  • Due to laminar flow: higher velocity at center → erythrocytes pushed toward center by rotary motion
  • Rouleau formation: Erythrocytes stack like coins due to flat discoid shape and large contact surface area
  • In vessels < 3 μm: viscosity increases, elasticity occurs (viscoelasticity)

Viscoelasticity

  • Blood is viscoelastic due to cellular components in plasma
  • In vessels < 3 μm: both viscosity and elasticity are present
  • Viscoelasticity decreases as shear rate (velocity) increases
